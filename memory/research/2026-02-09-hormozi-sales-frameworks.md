# Alex Hormozi Sales Frameworks Research
**Date**: 2026-02-09
**Context**: MTL Craft Cocktails email follow-up and lead nurturing strategy
**Sources**: $100M Offers, $100M Leads

## Key Frameworks

### 1. Grand Slam Offer
**Definition**: An offer so compelling customers feel stupid saying no.

**Core principle**: Create exceptional value by delivering outcomes while reducing perceived risks.

### 2. The Value Equation
```
VALUE = (Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood) ÷ (Time Delay × Effort & Sacrifice)
```

**Top of equation (creates value)**:
- Dream Outcome: The desired solution to their problem
- Perceived Likelihood: How believable is it they'll achieve the outcome

**Bottom of equation (destroys value)**:
- Time Delay: How long they wait for results
- Effort & Sacrifice: How much work they need to put in

**Key insight**: Most businesses focus on top of equation; magic is in reducing the bottom.

### 3. Lead Nurturing & Follow-Up

**Warm Outreach (Rule of 100)**:
- Contact 100 warm leads per day
- Expected metrics: 20% engage → 20% of those accept free offer → 25% of those convert to paid
- Dean Jackson's 9-word email: "Are you still looking to [4-word desire]?"
- Expected result: 100 contacts → 20 replies → 4 free offers → 1 customer

**Cold Outreach**:
- Minimum 100 people/day for 100 days
- Follow up multiple times (2-3x minimum)
- Each follow-up adds value or addresses objections
- Persistence is key; wait a few days between follow-ups

**Re-engaging Lost/Dead Leads**:
- Pattern interruption (Kevin Hart meme example)
- Avoid "just checking in" or "bumping to top of inbox"
- A-C-A framework: Acknowledge → Compliment → Ask
- Personal touch: congratulate on recent life event, then offer service

### 4. Scarcity & Urgency (Real, Not Manufactured)
- Must be based on real limitations or deadlines
- Honest implementation maintains trust
- Examples: Limited seats, time-bound availability
- Avoid artificial pressure tactics

**Critical perspective**: Hormozi's critics note his history of questionable tactics (trading driver's license for credit card). For professional service businesses, avoid anything that feels manipulative.

### 5. Pricing & Anchoring

**Price Anchoring**:
- Introduce premium package at 10-100x higher price
- Acts as mental benchmark making core offer seem reasonable
- Example: $25K premium package makes $5K package feel accessible

**Value-Based Pricing**:
- Price on value delivered, not cost-plus
- Customer should feel they receive far more value than price paid
- Higher price signals higher quality (especially B2B)

**Service Business Specific**:
- Charge more = forced to deliver more value = differentiation
- Focus on Lifetime Value (LTV)
- Measure: ability to deliver, adequate pricing, product quality for retention

## What to AVOID (Bro-Marketing Red Flags)

1. "Just checking in" emails
2. "Bumping to top of inbox" messages
3. Fake scarcity ("only 2 spots left" when unlimited)
4. Manipulative urgency tactics
5. Trading personal items for credit cards
6. Paywalling everything behind urgency
7. Words like "awesome," "crushing it," "insane"
8. Generic sales scripts that smell like funnel automation

## Application to Service Businesses (MTL Craft Cocktails)

### Value Equation for Cocktail Services

**Dream Outcome**:
- Guests talking about cocktails weeks later
- Host looking like a hero
- No bartending stress on event day

**Perceived Likelihood**:
- Professional mixologist credentials
- Photos/videos of past events
- Testimonials from similar events

**Time Delay** (reduce this):
- Instant quote within 24h
- Quick booking process
- Clear timeline communication

**Effort & Sacrifice** (reduce this):
- We handle everything (shopping, setup, cleanup)
- Flexible menu co-creation
- Bilingual service (no translation needed)

### Follow-Up Cadence (Adapted)

**Day 0**: Initial quote sent
**Day 3**: Value-add follow-up (not "checking in")
**Day 7**: Address common objection or share social proof
**Day 14**: Pattern interrupt or personal touch
**Day 30**: Re-engagement with new angle

### Re-engagement Angles for Lost Leads

1. **Seasonal hook**: "We just added summer cocktails for terrace season"
2. **Personal**: "Noticed you're planning [event type] — we just perfected a signature drink for those"
3. **Social proof**: "Just bartended for [similar client] — reminded me of your event"
4. **Value-add**: "Created a cocktail pairing guide for [their event type] — thought you'd find it useful"

### Natural Email Tone (Professional Bartender, Not Marketing Funnel)

**Good**:
- "We handle the shopping, setup, and cleanup"
- "Our mixologists work with you to design the menu"
- "We've bartended for 200+ events across Montreal"

**Avoid**:
- "This is an AMAZING opportunity"
- "Only 2 spots left this month" (unless true)
- "You're going to LOVE this"
- Excessive exclamation points
- Marketing automation voice

## Key Takeaway

Hormozi's frameworks work when adapted thoughtfully. For professional service businesses like MTL Craft Cocktails:
- Focus on reducing time delay and effort (bottom of value equation)
- Follow up with value, not pressure
- Use real scarcity only (actual calendar limitations)
- Price on transformation delivered, not hours worked
- Sound like a human who runs a cocktail business, not a marketing course graduate
