# Chris Do (The Futur) Sales Framework Research

**Research Date:** 2026-02-09
**Context:** Research for MTL Craft Cocktails email reply framework
**Goal:** Non-tacky, professional sales techniques for service business

## Core Philosophy

### 1. Amateurs Give Advice, Experts Diagnose
- Don't assume client needs or roadblocks
- Form all thinking into questions to diagnose the problem first
- Whoever asks the most questions controls the conversation
- Questions signal genuine interest and uncover valuable information

### 2. Sell Without Selling (Socratic Approach)
- Less about convincing arguments, more about strategic questions
- Question-based responses reframe objections
- Make it a collaborative conversation, not a pitch
- No high-pressure tactics or boiler room techniques

### 3. Value-Based Pricing Philosophy
- Price the client, not the work
- Value is determined by the client, not by you
- Charge based on value provided, not time spent
- You're selling clients their time back

## The Three Golden Questions

Chris Do's core framework for any client meeting:
1. **What are you trying to accomplish?**
2. **What's getting in the way of you achieving this goal?**
3. **Why do you believe this to be the case?**

## Question Types: Low vs High Value

### Low Value Questions (quantitative, fact-based):
- "What does it look like?"
- "When is it due?"
- "Who is the target market?"
- "What is the budget?"

### High Value Questions (qualitative, reflective):
- "How did you come up with this idea?"
- "How do you want people to feel?"
- "How will you judge if this is successful?"

**Why High Value Questions Matter:**
- Surface "purpose" and deeper motivations
- Cause people to be more reflective
- Help people/companies know themselves better
- Lead to better solutions and higher value work

## Handling "You're Too Expensive" Objections

### 1. Retreat and Follow
Response: **"I'm not sure you can afford us."**
- Shifts dynamic completely
- Client now has to prove they can afford you
- Advanced technique that reverses psychology

### 2. Embrace and Pivot
Response: **"I understand you have a certain budget in mind, and maybe we are too expensive."**
- Acknowledge their concern
- Don't fight it
- Pivot to value conversation

### 3. Double Down
- Challenge their position by agreeing with them
- Concede that cheaper options exist
- Question their underlying assumption
- Example: "You're right, there are cheaper options. What made you reach out to us?"

### 4. Price Bracketing/Anchoring
- Give a range instead of hard number
- Example: "Anywhere between $1,000-$3,000 depending on scope"
- Collect more information before narrowing
- Sets expectations early

### 5. Emphasize Value
- Remind them why they came to you
- Focus on the better solution they're seeking
- Tie back to quality, professionalism, results

## Expert Positioning (Not Order Taker)

### Key Principles:
- Be super specific about who you are, who you serve, why it matters
- Analyze strengths, boost expertise, determine positioning
- Be drastically and meaningfully different than competition
- Stop being an order taker and charge premium

### Discovery Before Solutions:
- Investigate and ask questions first
- Get to the bottom of client's problems before providing solutions
- Don't push solutions - ask what they feel is missing or could be improved
- Experts diagnose before prescribing

## The Socratic Six Framework

- Conversational framework based on 20+ years of techniques
- Question-based responses to client objections
- Composed approach instead of defensive reactions
- Don't need all the answers - just need right questions
- Listen to understand what prospects actually say
- Guide them to solution without high pressure

## Handling Emotional/Aggressive Clients

When client enters conversation in aggressive/emotional state:
1. Acknowledge their emotional state
2. Demonstrate empathy
3. Ask: **"May I ask, have you been burned before?"**
- Opens dialogue
- Shows understanding
- Reframes objection as opportunity for education

## Email/Messaging Best Practices

### DO:
- Ask more questions than you tell
- Make it collaborative, not pitchy
- Give something valuable
- Tell them something they didn't know
- Be present and delve deeper for clarity
- Use deep follow-up questions

### DON'T:
- Use high-pressure tactics
- Give advice before diagnosing
- Assume you know their needs
- Lead with price or features
- Sound like traditional "salesperson"

## Application to Mobile Bartending Business

### Natural Phrasings for MTL Craft Cocktails:
1. **Discovery:** "What are you hoping the bar experience will do for your event?"
2. **Qualification:** "What's most important to you - unique drinks, efficient service, or guest experience?"
3. **Objection Handling:** "I understand you might be comparing options. What made you reach out to us specifically?"
4. **Value Focus:** "How will you know if the bar service was a success?"

### AVOID from Chris Do's Teachings:
- Over-formalized language that sounds corporate
- Retreat and Follow in email (works in live conversation, sounds arrogant in text)
- Price bracketing in first email (better for discovery call)
- Any technique that sounds "salesy" or forced
- Corporate jargon or buzzwords

## Key Takeaway

Chris Do's approach is about **positioning yourself as the expert through questions, not claims**. For a mobile bartending business, this means asking about their event goals, guest experience, and success metrics - not leading with package details or trying to convince them you're the best. Let them discover your value through the conversation.
